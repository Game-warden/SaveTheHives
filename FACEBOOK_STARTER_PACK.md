**Long "About" section:**
> SaveTheHives is a free, volunteer-run citizen science project mapping non-managed honey bee colonies across North America. Over 1,150 legacy records dating back to 2008 are already on the map, and growing. Our focus: colonies that persist winter after winter with zero human treatment — what we call Genetic Goldmines — because each one is a real candidate for understanding how honey bees might survive without constant chemical support. Anyone can log a wild hive or confirm one that's already on the map, no experience or equipment required. savethehives.org
>
> *Scientific framing reviewed and approved by Dr. David Tarpy, NC State University Scholar Professor & Extension Apiculturist.*

## 5. Posting cadence (added Jul 21 2026, based on current nonprofit-page benchmarks)

For a page under 10,000 followers, 2–3 posts/week outperforms daily posting without a clear angle — quality and consistency beat volume. Best engagement windows for purpose-driven pages: **10am–4pm Mon–Wed**, plus **Sundays** for community/reflective stories.

Suggested weekly rhythm once launch week is done:
- **Monday (10am–2pm):** science/mission content — a research hook, a "why this matters" post.
- **Wednesday (10am–2pm):** practical/recruiting content — how-to, seasonal tips, bee ID, safety.
- **Sunday:** community/reflective — found-colony spotlights, milestones, "join us" calls to action.

## 6. Post ideas grounded in current (Jul 2026) research and citizen-science activity

Real, current hooks to draw on — each one ties back to the Genetic Goldmine thesis rather than being generic bee content:

- **Californian hybrid honeybees show natural Varroa resistance** (Sci.News, 2026) — mites are less attracted to their larvae at the exact developmental stage they normally invade. This is the science literally validating what SaveTheHives hunts for: wild, naturally resistant survivor stock. Strong share/press material.
- **USDA ARS: Varroa mites developing resistance to amitraz** (ars.usda.gov, Feb 2026) — explains why managed, treated colonies are on a chemical treadmill that keeps needing new products. Reinforces why untreated survivors are the more durable answer.
- **Spider-venom peptides as a novel, non-chemical Varroa control** (phys.org, Jul 2026) — a surprising, shareable science story; good engagement bait, loosely tied to "researchers are chasing alternatives to chemical treatment — the bees that already don't need any are worth finding."
- **Varroa Sensitive Hygiene (VSH) breeding programs** — managed breeding is trying to engineer the exact trait wild survivor colonies may already have naturally. Good "two paths to the same goal" framing.
- **2025–2026 U.S. Beekeeping Survey** (Auburn University + Apiary Inspectors of America) — a complementary citizen-science effort tracking managed-colony health nationally. Worth a "we're part of a bigger movement" post, possibly with a direct shoutout/tag — different data focus (managed colonies vs. non-managed), not a competitor.
- **The Bee Conservancy's "Bee the Solution"** and similar community-science-for-bees programs — same framing: SaveTheHives isn't the only citizen-science bee project, and name-checking peers lends credibility and puts you in front of hashtag-adjacent audiences (#CitizenScience, #BeeTheSolution).
- **World Bee Day (May 20)** — already passed for 2026, but its theme this year (collaboration among communities, institutions, farmers, and researchers) is exactly the partner-recruitment pitch. Worth calendaring for May 2027, and possibly a low-key "belated" mention now if it fits a post.
- **Citizen Science Month (April)** — SciStarter's annual flagship campaign. Another one to calendar ahead for next year rather than chase now.

## 7. Beyond Facebook — the larger strategy

Facebook is one channel; a few higher-leverage moves worth doing in parallel, not instead:

- **List SaveTheHives on SciStarter's Project Finder** (scistarter.org/finder) — a one-time setup step that puts the project in front of people actively searching for a citizen-science project to join, rather than people who have to stumble onto it. Low effort, durable payoff.
- **Reach out to the U.S. Beekeeping Survey team (Auburn/Apiary Inspectors of America) and/or The Bee Conservancy** — potential cross-promotion partners, not competitors, since their data focus (managed-colony health, broad pollinator advocacy) is different from SaveTheHives' non-managed-colony mapping.
- **The Researcher & Collaborator Brief** (item #12 in `CONTENT_LIBRARY_IDEAS.md`) is now unusually well-timed — there's active 2026 research on natural Varroa resistance, so academics and press are already primed for this exact story. Worth prioritizing once Dr. Tarpy's feedback on the Genetic Goldmine wording is in.
- **Light-touch Reddit presence** (r/beekeeping and similar) — as discussed earlier, self-promotion reads poorly there, but sharing one of the research stories above with SaveTheHives mentioned as relevant context is a better fit for that community's norms.
- **Regional beekeeping associations and NC State Extension** — still the highest-leverage single move for reach (one org sharing to their list beats slow organic growth), now backed by concrete research talking points instead of just the mission pitch alone.

## 9. Practical notes

- **Hashtags:** keep light on Facebook (unlike Instagram/X) — #SaveTheHives #CitizenScience #Beekeeping #Beelining is plenty.
- **Posting cadence:** 2–3x/week is sustainable for a volunteer-run page; don't over-promise a daily schedule.
- **Tone:** warm, plainspoken, not academic — matches the app and the beelining guide's existing voice. Avoid jargon; explain terms (Varroa, non-managed colony) briefly inline rather than assuming knowledge.

**Terminology note (added Jul 22 2026):** Dr. Tarpy mentioned the field is trending toward "non-managed" rather than "feral" to describe these colonies — updated throughout this doc and the mission explainer. Use "non-managed" going forward in new content.
- **Don't claim features that aren't live** — Genetic Goldmine badges, DCA mapping, and the Guardian Network are all marked "Coming soon" in the app. Describe them as mission/vision, not as something to go try today.

## 10. Post settings checklist (added Jul 22 2026 — applies to every post)

After pasting the caption, attaching the image, and setting alt text, Facebook shows a final "Post settings" screen before publishing. Defaults are correct for every post in this doc — no need to change any of these each time:

- **Post audience:** Public
- **Scheduling options:** Publish now (unless deliberately holding a post for its suggested date — in that case use the built-in scheduler rather than posting early and letting it sit)
- **Share to groups:** leave off by default. Group-sharing is a deliberate, separate move for specific beekeeping/community groups (see §7, "Beyond Facebook"), not something to attach to routine posts.
- **Share to story:** off — Stories aren't part of the current content plan.
- **Boost post:** always off — zero-budget project, no paid promotion.
