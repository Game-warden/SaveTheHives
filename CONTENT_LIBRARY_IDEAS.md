# SaveTheHives — Content Library Roadmap

A menu of documents and assets to grow the project's reach. The existing
`BEELINING_GUIDE.md` is the anchor; everything here either feeds visitors toward
it, extends it, or builds the broader mission around it.

---

## TIER 1 — Highest leverage (build these first)

### 1. The Beelining Bee Box — Free Printable Build Plan
**Audience:** makers, Scout troops, aspiring hunters · **Effort:** medium (needs a real, tested plan + a diagram)
The beelining guide points to this and it doesn't exist yet. A one-page PDF with cut list, dimensions, and an exploded diagram of the classic two-compartment box removes the single biggest barrier to trying beelining ("I don't have the tool"). Doubles as a shareable, printable handout at events and a natural Scout/STEM activity. High recruiting value, and it makes the guide's Module 6 complete.

### 2. "What Is a Genetic Goldmine?" — The Mission Explainer
**Audience:** everyone, especially press and donors · **Effort:** low (mostly writing)
One tight page on *why* wild survivor colonies matter: feral bees that live 3+ winters treatment-free are the untreated control group for disease-resistance research. This is the project's scientific soul and its best press hook ("Pokémon GO for bee conservation," the CCD control-group angle). Currently that story is scattered across the About panel and the spec — it deserves a single, quotable, standalone piece.

### 3. Social Media Starter Pack
**Audience:** you, for launch · **Effort:** low
A ready-to-post bundle: the five pull-quotes already in the beelining guide, plus ~10 more, matched to suggested visuals (a bee on a flower, a bee-line diagram, a "found it!" tree photo). Include 3–4 caption templates for Instagram/Facebook/Reddit and a short "what is this project" blurb in three lengths (one-liner, one-paragraph, one-screen). This is the drumming-up-interest kit you asked about, packaged for copy-paste.

### 4. First-Timer's Quick Start (One Page)
**Audience:** the visitor who says "ok, I'm in" · **Effort:** low (condense the guide)
A single-screen distillation of the beelining guide's Track 2: what to bring, the 8 steps, the one safety rule (leave it, log it), and a "your first hunt won't be perfect and that's fine" reassurance. The guide is the textbook; this is the cheat sheet people actually carry.

## TIER 2 — Strong supporting content

### 5. Seasonal Beelining Calendar
**Audience:** active hunters · **Effort:** low–medium
When and where to hunt through the year: late-summer/early-fall prime season, what's blooming (goldenrod, aster), regional variation. Pairs with a "log your find" push at peak season. Good recurring social content ("Beelining season starts now").

### 6. Reading the Landscape — Where Wild Colonies Live
**Audience:** intermediate hunters · **Effort:** medium
A field-craft piece on likely nest sites: hollow trees (which species, what cavity size), old buildings, chimneys, the sunny side of trunks, reading converging flight lines. Extends the guide's Module 7 "find the nest" step into its own skill.

### 7. Bee ID for Beeliners — Is That Even a Honey Bee?
**Audience:** beginners · **Effort:** medium (needs clear imagery)
Honey bee vs. bumblebee vs. wasp vs. the many solitary bees. Prevents wasted hunts chasing the wrong insect and doubles as broad pollinator-education content that draws a wider audience than just hunters.

### 8. The Waggle Dance — How Bees Beeline to Each Other
**Audience:** the curious, students · **Effort:** low–medium
A short, wonder-driven piece on von Frisch's Nobel-winning discovery, framed as "the bees have been beelining to each other all along." Pure engagement content; strong for schools and social. The beelining guide's Module 2 already gestures at this — this expands it.

### 9. Field Safety & Ethics Card
**Audience:** everyone · **Effort:** low
The guide's Module 5 as a standalone, printable card: leave the colony, know whose land you're on, don't handle wild nests, Africanized-bee caution, tell someone where you went. Liability-smart to have as its own artifact people can be pointed to.

## TIER 3 — Depth, community & scale

### 10. Scout & Classroom Activity Kit
**Audience:** troop leaders, teachers · **Effort:** medium–high
Beelining as a STEM unit — it combines biology (bee behavior), geometry (triangulation), and orienteering. Merit-badge-style worksheet, a simplified box plan (item #1), and a data-logging exercise that feeds real records into SaveTheHives. Directly serves the Scout-integration idea already in the project's outreach notes.

### 11. Landowner One-Pager ("You Have Wild Bees — That's Good News")
**Audience:** property owners a hunter needs to approach · **Effort:** low
A friendly explainer a hunter can hand (or email) to a landowner: what the project is, why their wild colony matters, that nobody will disturb it, and how they can opt in. Turns the trespass/permission friction from Module 5 into a recruiting moment.

### 12. Researcher & Collaborator Brief
**Audience:** scientists, institutions · **Effort:** medium
A credible one-pager on the dataset (what's collected, coverage, the survivor-colony focus), data-access options, and collaboration paths. Supports the Dr. Tarpy relationship and any future academic partnerships. More formal register than the public content.

### 13. "How Pathfinder Works (and Why It's Hard)" — Dev-Facing Explainer
**Audience:** technical contributors · **Effort:** low (adapt existing docs)
A public-friendly version of the Pathfinder handoff: the triangulation physics, why phone sensors limit accuracy, an invitation to help. Recruits technical volunteers and sets honest expectations. Draws directly from `PATHFINDER_HANDOFF.md`.

### 14. Success Stories / Found-Colony Gallery
**Audience:** everyone · **Effort:** low, but ongoing
A living page of real finds — photo, short story, what made it interesting (a 5-winter survivor, an odd nest site). Social proof is the best recruiter; every logged colony is potential content. Start small, grow it as records come in.

### 15. Elevate Validate as the Primary On-Ramp (raised Jul 17 2026)
**Audience:** first-time/casual visitors · **Effort:** low-medium (UI reframing + copy, no new backend)
Right now Validate (v2.9) is just another bottom-nav tab, discovered the same way as Add. But it's a fundamentally easier ask: confirming a hive that's already on the map (tap, "still active?", done) takes under a minute and requires no field skill, versus Add or the Learn-tab tracks, which ask someone to go find and log a brand-new wild colony. That asymmetry is worth designing around deliberately rather than leaving Validate to be discovered incidentally.

**Why this matters beyond engagement:** every stale record that gets reconfirmed (or flagged gone) directly improves the dataset researchers care about — a colony verified active across multiple years is exactly the "genetic goldmine" survivor signal the project's science story is built on (see item #2). Validate turns casual visitors into unpaid, distributed data-quality reviewers, which no comparable citizen-science mapping project (HoneyBeeWatch, iNaturalist-style feral-species trackers, etc.) appears to be doing explicitly for existing records — most only solicit new submissions. **This may be a genuinely novel mechanic worth leading with, not burying in the nav.**

Concrete moves (see the mockups from the Jul 17 2026 session for visual direction):
- Reframe the first-visit on-ramp overlay (`#onramp-overlay` in `index.html`) to lead with Validate, not just "Explore the map" / "How it works" — e.g., a stat-driven hook ("╳ hives near you haven't been checked in years — confirm one in under a minute") with Validate as the primary button.
- A persistent, low-key nudge for returning visitors who already dismissed the on-ramp — a small badge/banner surfacing a live stale-hive count near the visitor, separate from the one-time overlay.
- A fourth "path card" or explicit mention on the Learn hub (alongside Curious/Try-it/Maker) positioning Validate as the lowest-effort way to help — the funnel currently only points toward logging new hives.
- Lightweight social proof once volume exists: a running count of hives validated (e.g., "confirmed by 40 citizen scientists this month").

Not yet built — UI mockups reviewed, no code changed.

## A few cross-cutting notes

- **Reuse, don't duplicate.** Most Tier-1/2 items are re-cuts of the beelining guide's modules at different lengths for different audiences. Write the fact once (in the guide), express it many ways. Keep the guide as the source of truth so facts don't drift.
- **Every public piece should end in one action:** *log a hive*, *validate one that's already logged*, *build a box*, or *share this*. Content that doesn't funnel toward contributing is just decoration. Validate (added v2.9) is worth treating as its own funnel destination, not folded silently into "log a hive" — see item #15.
- **Visual debt is the real bottleneck.** Much of this needs diagrams/photos, not prose. The `[DIAGRAM: ...]` callouts in the beelining guide are a starting shot list; a small set of clean SVGs in the app's honey/dark palette would lift everything.
- **Sequencing suggestion:** items 1, 2, 3 give you a launch kit (a tool plan, a mission story, and a posting pack). That trio is enough to start drumming up interest immediately; the rest can follow as the community grows.
