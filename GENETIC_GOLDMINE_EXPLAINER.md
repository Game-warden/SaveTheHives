# What Is a Genetic Goldmine?

*Scientific framing reviewed and approved by Dr. David Tarpy, NC State University Scholar Professor & Extension Apiculturist — Jul 21, 2026.*

Somewhere near you, there's almost certainly a colony of honey bees living wild — no beekeeper, no treatments, no help. It's been there for years. And it might be one of the most valuable bees in America.

## The problem

Honey bees are in trouble. Since the mid-2000s, U.S. beekeepers have lost roughly 30–50% of their managed colonies every single year — to Varroa mites, the viruses those mites spread, pesticides, and habitat loss. Most surviving colonies only make it because beekeepers treat them constantly: miticides, supplemental feeding, careful management. Take the treatments away, and most managed bees die within a couple of winters.

But not all of them.

## The goldmine

Scattered across forests, old barns, and hollow trees are non-managed honey bee colonies that survive winter after winter with no mite treatments and no human management. A colony that persists three or more seasons on its own isn't just lucky — it's come through real, sustained natural selection from Varroa mites and the viruses they spread. That persistence may come from heritable resistance, from behavior, or from favorable local ecology — and untangling which one is exactly the kind of question that needs living colonies to study.

That's why SaveTheHives calls these long-lived non-managed colonies **Genetic Goldmines**: not because the genetics are proven, but because each one is a real candidate for understanding how honey bees might survive without constant chemical support.

## Why a map matters

Nobody currently tracks where these survivor colonies are. They're not in a registry. Most are found by accident, by a hiker or a beekeeper chasing a swarm, and then forgotten. SaveTheHives exists to fix that: a crowdsourced map where anyone — a Scout troop, a retired beekeeper, a curious neighbor — can log a wild colony, and where returning visitors can confirm whether it's still alive months or years later.

The tool for finding these colonies in the first place is nearly 300 years old: **beelining**, the old craft of following a fed bee's flight path straight back to its nest. It's part treasure hunt, part orienteering, and it turns an afternoon walk into a real data point. No experience needed, no bees to own — just a compass, patience, and an eye for a hollow tree.

## The pitch, short version

Every wild colony that survives on its own, untreated, is doing an experiment nature has been running for free. SaveTheHives just needs people willing to go find it, log it, and check back in. Every pin on the map is a lead. Every "still alive" check-in is data no lab could otherwise get.
